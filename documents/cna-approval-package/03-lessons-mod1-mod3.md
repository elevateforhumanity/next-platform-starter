# MODULE 1: Introduction to the CNA Role

## Lesson 1.1 — The Role and Scope of Practice of a CNA

**Learning Objective:** Students will be able to define the certified nursing assistant role, identify the legal scope of practice in Indiana, and describe the CNA's position within the healthcare team.

**Key Terms:** Scope of practice, delegation, interdisciplinary team, Indiana Nurse Aide Registry, OBRA 1987

**Instructional Content:**

A Certified Nursing Assistant (CNA) is a trained healthcare worker who provides direct patient care under the supervision of a licensed nurse. The CNA role is one of the most important in healthcare because CNAs spend more direct time with patients than any other member of the care team. Understanding what you are authorized to do — and what you are not — is the foundation of safe, legal, and ethical practice.

In Indiana, the CNA scope of practice is governed by Indiana Code 16-28-13 and 410 IAC 17. These regulations define the minimum training requirements, the competency evaluation process, and the standards for maintaining listing on the Indiana Nurse Aide Registry. Every CNA in Indiana must be listed on this registry before working in a Medicare- or Medicaid-certified facility.

The scope of practice for a CNA includes: assisting patients with activities of daily living (ADLs) such as bathing, dressing, grooming, and toileting; measuring and recording vital signs; assisting with mobility and transfers; providing skin care and pressure injury prevention; serving meals and assisting with feeding; observing and reporting changes in patient condition; and maintaining a clean and safe environment.

CNAs do NOT administer medications, perform sterile procedures, insert or remove catheters, interpret diagnostic results, or make medical decisions. These tasks fall within the scope of licensed nurses and physicians. If a nurse asks you to perform a task outside your scope, you are legally and ethically required to decline and report the request.

The healthcare team includes the physician or advanced practice provider, registered nurse (RN), licensed practical nurse (LPN), physical therapist, occupational therapist, speech-language pathologist, social worker, dietitian, and the CNA. The RN holds supervisory authority over the CNA and is responsible for delegating tasks appropriately. Delegation is the process by which a licensed nurse assigns a specific task to a CNA after determining that the task is within the CNA's training and that the patient's condition is stable enough for the task to be safely performed by a non-licensed worker.

HIPAA — the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — requires that all patient information be kept strictly confidential. This includes the patient's name, diagnosis, treatment, room number, and any other identifying information. CNAs must never discuss patient information in hallways, elevators, or public spaces. Social media posts about patients, even without names, are a HIPAA violation and grounds for termination and legal action.

**Practical Application:** In pairs, students will role-play a scenario in which a family member asks a CNA for information about a patient's diagnosis. Students will practice responding professionally while maintaining HIPAA compliance.

## Lesson 1.2 — Professionalism, Ethics, and the Indiana Nurse Aide Registry

**Learning Objective:** Students will describe professional conduct standards for CNAs, explain the Indiana Nurse Aide Registry requirements, and identify behaviors that result in registry removal.

**Key Terms:** Professionalism, ethics, Indiana Nurse Aide Registry, misconduct, in-service training

**Instructional Content:**

Professionalism is not just about how you dress or speak — it is a commitment to putting the patient's wellbeing above your own comfort, preferences, or personal opinions. As a CNA, you are often the first person a patient sees in the morning and the last person they see at night. Your attitude, reliability, and compassion directly affect patient outcomes and quality of life.

Professional conduct includes arriving on time and in proper uniform, maintaining a clean and neat appearance, addressing patients by their preferred name and title, following the chain of command when concerns arise, completing all assigned tasks thoroughly and on time, and treating every patient with equal dignity regardless of their background, diagnosis, or behavior.

Ethical conduct means doing the right thing even when no one is watching. CNAs face ethical situations regularly: a patient asks you not to tell the nurse about a symptom, a coworker cuts corners on care, or a family member offers you a gift. In each case, your ethical obligation is clear — report symptoms, maintain standards, and decline gifts per facility policy.

The Indiana Nurse Aide Registry is maintained by the Indiana State Department of Health. To be listed, a CNA must complete an approved training program and pass the Indiana Nurse Aide Competency Evaluation, which includes a written exam and a skills demonstration. Once listed, CNAs must work at least eight hours of paid nursing-related work every 24 months to maintain active status. CNAs must also complete in-service training as required by their employer — typically 12 hours per year in long-term care settings.

Registry removal can occur for substantiated findings of abuse, neglect, exploitation, or misappropriation of patient property. These findings are investigated by the Indiana State Department of Health and, if substantiated, result in permanent listing on the registry as "not in good standing," which bars the individual from working in any Medicare- or Medicaid-certified facility in the United States.

**Practical Application:** Students will review a case study involving a CNA who witnesses a coworker being rough with a patient. Students will identify the ethical obligation, the correct reporting chain, and the potential consequences of failing to report.

# MODULE 2: Infection Control & Standard Precautions

## Lesson 2.1 — The Chain of Infection and Medical Asepsis

**Learning Objective:** Students will identify the six links in the chain of infection and describe specific actions that break each link to prevent the spread of infectious disease.

**Key Terms:** Chain of infection, infectious agent, reservoir, portal of exit, mode of transmission, portal of entry, susceptible host, medical asepsis, surgical asepsis

**Instructional Content:**

Infection control is one of the most critical competencies a CNA must master. Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) affect approximately 1 in 31 hospital patients on any given day in the United States, according to the CDC. Many of these infections are preventable through consistent application of infection control principles. As a CNA, your hands are your most important tool — and your most common vehicle for spreading infection.

The chain of infection describes the six conditions that must all be present for an infection to spread from one person to another. Breaking any single link in this chain stops the infection.

**Link 1 — Infectious Agent:** The microorganism that causes disease. This includes bacteria (Staphylococcus aureus, Clostridium difficile), viruses (influenza, COVID-19, norovirus), fungi (Candida), and parasites. Each agent has different characteristics that affect how it spreads and how it is killed.

**Link 2 — Reservoir:** The place where the infectious agent lives and multiplies. Reservoirs include the human body (skin, respiratory tract, GI tract), animals, soil, water, and contaminated equipment. In healthcare, the most common reservoir is the patient's own body or contaminated surfaces.

**Link 3 — Portal of Exit:** The route by which the agent leaves the reservoir. Common portals of exit include respiratory secretions (coughing, sneezing), blood and body fluids, feces, urine, wound drainage, and skin cells.

**Link 4 — Mode of Transmission:** How the agent travels from the reservoir to a new host. The four main modes are: contact transmission (direct touch or touching contaminated surfaces), droplet transmission (large respiratory droplets that travel up to 3 feet), airborne transmission (small particles that remain suspended in air), and vehicle transmission (contaminated food, water, or equipment).

**Link 5 — Portal of Entry:** The route by which the agent enters a new host. Portals of entry include the mouth, nose, eyes, broken skin, urinary tract, and any invasive device such as a catheter or IV line.

**Link 6 — Susceptible Host:** A person who lacks immunity to the infectious agent. Factors that increase susceptibility include advanced age, chronic illness, malnutrition, immunosuppression, recent surgery, and invasive devices.

Medical asepsis — also called "clean technique" — refers to practices that reduce the number and spread of microorganisms. This includes hand hygiene, wearing gloves, cleaning surfaces, and handling soiled linens properly. Surgical asepsis — "sterile technique" — eliminates all microorganisms and is used for invasive procedures. CNAs practice medical asepsis; sterile technique is performed by licensed nurses.

**Practical Application:** Students will trace the chain of infection through a scenario involving a patient with C. difficile and identify every point at which a CNA could break the chain during routine care.

## Lesson 2.2 — Hand Hygiene and PPE

**Learning Objective:** Students will demonstrate correct hand hygiene technique using soap and water and alcohol-based hand rub, and correctly don and doff personal protective equipment in the proper sequence.

**Key Terms:** Hand hygiene, alcohol-based hand rub, personal protective equipment (PPE), donning, doffing, contact precautions, droplet precautions, airborne precautions

**Instructional Content:**

Hand hygiene is the single most effective action a healthcare worker can take to prevent the spread of infection. The World Health Organization identifies five moments for hand hygiene: before touching a patient, before a clean or aseptic procedure, after body fluid exposure risk, after touching a patient, and after touching patient surroundings. CNAs should perform hand hygiene at every one of these moments without exception.

**Soap and Water Technique:** Wet hands with warm water. Apply enough soap to cover all surfaces. Rub hands together vigorously for at least 20 seconds, covering all surfaces including the backs of hands, between fingers, and under fingernails. Rinse thoroughly under running water. Dry with a single-use paper towel. Use the paper towel to turn off the faucet. Soap and water is required when hands are visibly soiled, before eating, after using the restroom, and when caring for patients with C. difficile or norovirus (alcohol does not kill these organisms).

**Alcohol-Based Hand Rub Technique:** Apply the product to the palm of one hand. Rub hands together, covering all surfaces of hands and fingers, until hands are dry (approximately 20–30 seconds). Do not rinse. Alcohol-based hand rub is acceptable when hands are not visibly soiled and is preferred for most routine patient care moments because it is faster and less damaging to skin.

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):** PPE creates a barrier between the healthcare worker and infectious material. The type of PPE required depends on the transmission-based precaution category.

Contact Precautions (e.g., MRSA, C. diff, VRE): Gown and gloves required upon entering the room.
Droplet Precautions (e.g., influenza, COVID-19, pertussis): Surgical mask required within 3 feet of patient. Gown and gloves as indicated.
Airborne Precautions (e.g., tuberculosis, measles, chickenpox): N95 respirator required. Patient in negative pressure room.

**Donning Sequence (putting on PPE):** Gown first → mask or respirator → eye protection → gloves last.
**Doffing Sequence (removing PPE):** Gloves first → eye protection → gown → mask last. Remove gloves by peeling from the wrist, turning inside out. Perform hand hygiene after each step and immediately after all PPE is removed.

The doffing sequence is critical because the outside of PPE is contaminated. Touching your face or clothing with contaminated gloves before removing them defeats the entire purpose of wearing PPE.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice donning and doffing a full set of PPE (gown, gloves, mask, eye protection) three times until the sequence is automatic. Instructor will observe and provide corrective feedback.

# MODULE 3: Safety, Body Mechanics & Emergency Procedures

## Lesson 3.1 — Body Mechanics and Ergonomic Safety

**Learning Objective:** Students will apply correct body mechanics principles during patient care activities to prevent musculoskeletal injury to themselves and patients.

**Key Terms:** Body mechanics, base of support, center of gravity, alignment, ergonomics, musculoskeletal injury

**Instructional Content:**

Musculoskeletal injuries are the leading cause of workplace injury among healthcare workers. Back injuries, in particular, are responsible for significant lost work time and long-term disability among nursing assistants. The good news is that most of these injuries are preventable through consistent application of correct body mechanics.

Body mechanics refers to the way we use our bodies to perform physical tasks safely and efficiently. The principles of body mechanics are based on physics — specifically, how to move weight with the least amount of strain on the spine and joints.

**Principle 1 — Maintain a Wide Base of Support:** Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. This increases your stability and reduces the risk of losing your balance during a transfer or lift. Think of a tripod — the wider the base, the more stable the structure.

**Principle 2 — Keep Your Center of Gravity Low:** Bend at the hips and knees, not at the waist. When you bend at the waist with straight legs, all the force of the load is transmitted directly to the lumbar spine. When you bend at the knees, your leg muscles — the largest and strongest muscles in the body — absorb the load instead.

**Principle 3 — Keep the Load Close to Your Body:** The further a load is from your center of gravity, the more force is required to hold it. When transferring a patient, bring them as close to your body as possible before moving. When carrying supplies, hold them against your chest rather than extended in front of you.

**Principle 4 — Avoid Twisting:** Twisting the spine while bearing a load is one of the most common causes of back injury. Instead of twisting, pivot your entire body by moving your feet. Point your feet in the direction you want to move before you begin the movement.

**Principle 5 — Push, Don't Pull:** When moving equipment such as wheelchairs, beds, or carts, push rather than pull whenever possible. Pushing uses larger muscle groups and keeps the load in front of you where you can see it.

**Principle 6 — Get Help:** No patient transfer should be performed alone if there is any doubt about safety. Use mechanical lifts, transfer belts, slide boards, and additional staff as needed. Asking for help is not a sign of weakness — it is a sign of professional judgment.

**Ergonomics in the Healthcare Setting:** Ergonomics is the science of designing work tasks to fit the worker. In healthcare, ergonomic strategies include adjusting bed height to working height before providing care, using ceiling lifts or floor lifts instead of manual transfers, using slide sheets to reposition patients in bed, and organizing the work environment to minimize reaching and bending.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice the correct technique for making an occupied bed, performing a stand-pivot transfer, and repositioning a patient in bed, with the instructor observing and correcting body mechanics in real time.

## Lesson 3.2 — Fall Prevention and Emergency Response

**Learning Objective:** Students will identify fall risk factors, describe fall prevention interventions, and demonstrate the correct response to a patient fall, choking emergency, and fire emergency.

**Key Terms:** Fall risk, fall prevention, incident report, RACE, PASS, Heimlich maneuver, aspiration

**Instructional Content:**

Falls are the most common adverse event in healthcare settings. In long-term care facilities, residents fall an average of 1.5 times per year. Falls cause fractures, head injuries, loss of confidence, and death. CNAs are on the front line of fall prevention because they spend the most time with patients and are most likely to observe fall risk factors.

**Fall Risk Factors:** Advanced age, history of previous falls, muscle weakness, gait or balance problems, cognitive impairment, medications (especially sedatives, antihypertensives, and diuretics), vision or hearing impairment, incontinence (patients rushing to the bathroom), and environmental hazards such as wet floors, poor lighting, and cluttered pathways.

**Fall Prevention Interventions:** Answer call lights promptly — most falls happen when patients try to get up without assistance because no one responded. Keep the bed in the lowest position with wheels locked when not providing care. Ensure non-slip footwear is worn at all times. Keep frequently used items within reach. Use bed alarms for high-risk patients. Offer toileting assistance every two hours. Ensure adequate lighting, especially at night. Remove clutter from pathways.

**Responding to a Fall:** If you find a patient on the floor or witness a fall, do NOT attempt to catch the patient — you will injure yourself and may injure the patient further. Instead: stay calm, call for help immediately, do not move the patient until a nurse assesses them, stay with the patient and provide reassurance, observe and report any injuries, loss of consciousness, or complaints of pain. After the nurse assesses the patient and determines it is safe to move them, assist with returning the patient to bed or chair. Complete an incident report as directed by your supervisor.

**Fire Safety — RACE and PASS:** In a fire emergency, remember RACE: Rescue anyone in immediate danger. Alarm — activate the nearest fire alarm pull station and call 911. Contain the fire by closing all doors. Extinguish the fire if it is small and you are trained, or Evacuate if the fire cannot be contained. To use a fire extinguisher, remember PASS: Pull the pin. Aim at the base of the fire. Squeeze the handle. Sweep side to side.

**Choking Response:** If a patient is choking and cannot speak, cough, or breathe, perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver): stand behind the patient, make a fist with one hand and place it thumb-side against the abdomen just above the navel and below the breastbone, grasp your fist with the other hand, and deliver firm upward thrusts until the object is expelled or the patient loses consciousness. If the patient loses consciousness, lower them to the floor, call for help, and begin CPR if trained.

**Practical Application:** Students will walk through a simulated fall response scenario and a fire evacuation drill, demonstrating correct RACE sequence and documentation of an incident report.
