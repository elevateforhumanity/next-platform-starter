# MODULE 7: Mobility, Transfers & Positioning

## Lesson 7.1 — Positioning and Pressure Injury Prevention

**Learning Objective:** Students will position patients in all required positions, explain the purpose of each, and implement a repositioning schedule to prevent pressure injuries.

**Key Terms:** Fowler's position, semi-Fowler's, lateral position, Sims' position, prone, supine, pressure injury, repositioning schedule, pressure-relieving device

**Instructional Content:**

Immobility is one of the greatest threats to a patient's health. When a patient cannot move independently, the CNA is responsible for ensuring that position changes occur regularly, that bony prominences are protected, and that the patient's body is aligned correctly to prevent contractures, nerve damage, and skin breakdown.

**Supine Position (lying on back):** The patient lies flat on their back. Ensure the spine is aligned, a small pillow supports the head and neck, and heels are elevated off the mattress using a pillow placed under the calves (not under the heels, which increases pressure). This position is used for many care procedures but should not be maintained for extended periods in patients at risk for pressure injuries or aspiration.

**Prone Position (lying on stomach):** The patient lies face down. This position is rarely used in long-term care but may be ordered for specific respiratory conditions. Ensure the face is turned to one side, a pillow is placed under the abdomen to reduce lumbar strain, and feet hang over the edge of the mattress to prevent foot drop.

**Lateral Position (side-lying):** The patient lies on their side. Place a pillow between the knees to prevent pressure on bony prominences and maintain hip alignment. Ensure the lower shoulder is pulled slightly forward to prevent it from being compressed under the body. This is the most common repositioning position and should alternate between left and right lateral.

**Fowler's Position:** The head of the bed is elevated 45–60 degrees. This position is used for eating, receiving medications, and patients with respiratory difficulty. Semi-Fowler's is 30–45 degrees and is used for patients who cannot tolerate full Fowler's.

**Sims' Position:** The patient lies on their left side with the right knee drawn up toward the chest. This position is used for enemas, rectal examinations, and patients who are unconscious (reduces aspiration risk).

**Repositioning Schedule:** Patients who cannot reposition themselves must be turned and repositioned at minimum every two hours. Document each position change on the repositioning log. Use pressure-relieving devices — foam overlays, alternating pressure mattresses, heel protectors — as ordered. These devices reduce pressure but do not eliminate the need for repositioning.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice positioning a mannequin in all five positions, demonstrating correct pillow placement, body alignment, and documentation of a repositioning log entry.

## Lesson 7.2 — Transfers, Ambulation, and Range of Motion

**Learning Objective:** Students will perform a stand-pivot transfer using a gait belt, assist with ambulation, operate a mechanical lift, and perform passive range of motion exercises for all major joints.

**Key Terms:** Gait belt, stand-pivot transfer, mechanical lift, ambulation, passive range of motion, contracture, dorsiflexion, plantar flexion

**Instructional Content:**

Transfers are among the highest-risk activities a CNA performs — both for patient safety and for the CNA's own musculoskeletal health. Every transfer requires assessment before execution: Is the patient able to bear weight? Is the patient cooperative and able to follow instructions? Is the environment clear of hazards? Is the correct equipment available?

**Gait Belt Application:** The gait belt is a safety device used during transfers and ambulation to provide a secure grip on the patient. Apply the belt around the patient's waist over their clothing, not over bare skin or an IV site. The belt should be snug enough that you can slide two fingers underneath it but not loose enough to slide up over the ribs. Grasp the belt with an underhand grip (palm up) during transfers.

**Stand-Pivot Transfer (Bed to Wheelchair):**
Position the wheelchair at a 45-degree angle to the bed on the patient's stronger side. Lock the wheelchair wheels. Raise the footrests. Assist the patient to a sitting position at the edge of the bed (dangle position) and allow them to sit for 30–60 seconds to prevent orthostatic hypotension. Apply the gait belt. Place your feet outside the patient's feet to block them. Bend your knees. On a count of three, assist the patient to stand by pulling upward on the gait belt while the patient pushes up with their hands. Once standing, pivot together toward the wheelchair. Lower the patient into the chair by bending your knees as they sit. Remove the gait belt. Lower the footrests and position the patient's feet.

**Mechanical Lift Operation:** A mechanical lift (Hoyer lift) is used for patients who cannot bear weight or who are too heavy for a manual transfer. Select the correct sling size and type. Position the sling under the patient while they are in bed (roll the patient side to side to position the sling). Attach the sling to the lift bar using the correct attachment points. Slowly raise the patient, ensuring the sling is secure and the patient is comfortable. Move the lift to the wheelchair. Lower the patient into the chair. Detach the sling from the lift bar. The sling may remain under the patient if they will be transferred back to bed within a short time.

**Ambulation Assistance:** Walk on the patient's weaker side, slightly behind them. Hold the gait belt with one hand. Encourage the patient to look forward, not down at their feet. If the patient begins to fall, do not try to hold them up — guide them to the floor by bending your knees and lowering them gently, protecting their head.

**Passive Range of Motion (PROM):** PROM exercises move each joint through its full range of motion to prevent contractures, maintain circulation, and preserve joint flexibility. Perform PROM slowly and gently — never force a joint beyond its comfortable range. Support the joint above and below the area being exercised. Perform each movement 3–5 times per joint per session.

Movements by joint: Neck (flexion, extension, rotation — only if ordered), shoulder (flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, internal/external rotation), elbow (flexion, extension), wrist (flexion, extension, radial/ulnar deviation), fingers (flexion, extension, abduction), hip (flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, internal/external rotation), knee (flexion, extension), ankle (dorsiflexion, plantar flexion), toes (flexion, extension).

**Practical Application:** Students will perform a complete stand-pivot transfer and a full passive ROM sequence on a mannequin, with the instructor evaluating technique using the skills competency checklist.

# MODULE 8: Nutrition, Hydration & Elimination

## Lesson 8.1 — Nutrition, Meal Assistance, and Fluid Monitoring

**Learning Objective:** Students will assist patients with meals using correct technique, monitor and record fluid intake and output, and recognize signs of dehydration and malnutrition.

**Key Terms:** Dysphagia, aspiration, therapeutic diet, fluid intake, fluid output, dehydration, malnutrition, NPO

**Instructional Content:**

Adequate nutrition and hydration are fundamental to healing, immune function, wound repair, and quality of life. CNAs play a critical role in ensuring patients receive adequate nutrition by assisting with meals, monitoring intake, and reporting concerns to the charge nurse.

**Therapeutic Diets:** Many patients are on modified diets ordered by the physician or dietitian. Common therapeutic diets include: regular (no restrictions), low-sodium (for hypertension and heart failure), diabetic/consistent carbohydrate (for diabetes management), low-fat (for cardiovascular disease), renal diet (restricted potassium, phosphorus, and protein for kidney disease), pureed (for patients with chewing difficulties), and mechanical soft (for patients with some chewing ability but difficulty with hard foods).

**Dysphagia and Aspiration Risk:** Dysphagia is difficulty swallowing. It is common after stroke, in patients with Parkinson's disease, and in advanced dementia. Aspiration occurs when food or liquid enters the airway instead of the esophagus, leading to aspiration pneumonia — a serious and potentially fatal complication. Signs of dysphagia include coughing or choking during meals, a wet or gurgly voice after eating, food pocketing in the cheeks, and drooling. If you observe these signs, stop the meal and report immediately.

Patients with dysphagia are often ordered thickened liquids (nectar-thick or honey-thick consistency) to slow the flow of liquid and reduce aspiration risk. Never give a patient thin liquids if they are ordered thickened liquids.

**Meal Assistance Technique:** Position the patient upright at 90 degrees (or as close as possible) before and during the meal. Remain seated at eye level with the patient. Offer small bites and allow adequate time for chewing and swallowing between bites. Alternate solids and liquids. Never rush a meal. Engage the patient in conversation. Keep the patient upright for at least 30 minutes after the meal to reduce reflux and aspiration risk.

**Fluid Intake and Output (I&O):** When I&O is ordered, every fluid the patient takes in and every fluid the patient puts out must be measured and recorded. Intake includes all oral fluids, IV fluids, tube feedings, and fluids given with medications. Output includes urine, emesis, wound drainage, and liquid stool. Urine output is measured in milliliters using a graduated container (hat) placed in the toilet or bedpan. Normal urine output is approximately 30 mL per hour or 720 mL per 8-hour shift. Output below 30 mL/hour must be reported immediately.

**Dehydration Signs:** Dry mouth and lips, dark concentrated urine, decreased urine output, sunken eyes, poor skin turgor (skin tents when pinched), confusion, dizziness, rapid weak pulse, and low blood pressure. Dehydration is a medical emergency in older adults and must be reported immediately.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice meal assistance technique with a classmate, measure and record simulated fluid intake and output, and identify dehydration signs from a case study.

# MODULE 9: Dementia, Mental Health & Restorative Care

## Lesson 9.1 — Dementia Care and Behavioral Approaches

**Learning Objective:** Students will describe the stages of Alzheimer's disease, apply person-centered dementia care techniques, and demonstrate de-escalation strategies for common behavioral symptoms.

**Key Terms:** Alzheimer's disease, dementia, sundowning, wandering, validation therapy, redirection, person-centered care, behavioral symptom

**Instructional Content:**

Dementia is not a normal part of aging — it is a disease that progressively destroys brain cells, affecting memory, thinking, behavior, and the ability to perform daily activities. Alzheimer's disease accounts for 60–80% of dementia cases. As a CNA in long-term care, you will care for many patients with dementia, and your approach to their care will profoundly affect their quality of life.

**Stages of Alzheimer's Disease:**

Early stage: The person may still live independently but experiences memory lapses, difficulty finding words, and challenges with planning or problem-solving. They are often aware of their diagnosis and may experience depression or anxiety.

Middle stage: This is the longest stage. The person requires increasing assistance with ADLs, may become confused about time and place, may not recognize family members, and may exhibit behavioral symptoms such as agitation, wandering, and sundowning. This is the stage when most people move to long-term care.

Late stage: The person loses the ability to communicate verbally, requires full assistance with all ADLs, loses mobility, and becomes vulnerable to infections. Care focuses on comfort, dignity, and pain management.

**Person-Centered Dementia Care:** Person-centered care means seeing the person behind the disease — their history, preferences, values, and personality — and using that knowledge to guide care. Before providing care, learn about the patient: What did they do for work? What music do they love? What are their daily routines? This information, gathered from family and care plans, allows you to connect with the person in a meaningful way.

**Communication Strategies:** Use the patient's name. Approach from the front and identify yourself every time. Use short, simple sentences. Speak slowly and calmly. Maintain eye contact. Use a gentle touch on the hand or arm to establish connection. Never argue, correct, or quiz the patient about facts they cannot remember.

**Validation Therapy:** Rather than correcting a patient's false belief, validate the emotion behind it. If a patient says "I need to go home to cook dinner for my husband," do not say "Your husband passed away ten years ago." Instead, say "It sounds like you love taking care of your family. Tell me about your husband." This approach reduces distress and preserves dignity.

**Sundowning:** Many patients with dementia become more confused, agitated, or restless in the late afternoon and evening. Strategies to reduce sundowning include maintaining a consistent daily routine, ensuring adequate daytime activity and light exposure, reducing noise and stimulation in the evening, offering a light snack, and playing familiar calming music.

**Wandering:** Wandering is a safety risk. Prevention strategies include door alarms, secured exits, visual barriers (a stop sign or curtain over a door), identification bracelets, and structured walking programs that provide safe outlets for the need to move.

**Practical Application:** Students will role-play three dementia care scenarios: a patient who is refusing a bath, a patient who is sundowning and agitated, and a patient who believes they need to go to work. Students will demonstrate validation, redirection, and de-escalation techniques.

# MODULE 10: Chronic Conditions & End-of-Life Care

## Lesson 10.1 — Common Chronic Conditions

**Learning Objective:** Students will identify the CNA's care responsibilities for patients with diabetes, COPD, CHF, and stroke, and recognize signs of acute deterioration requiring immediate reporting.

**Key Terms:** Diabetes, hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia, COPD, dyspnea, CHF, edema, stroke, hemiplegia, aphasia

**Instructional Content:**

The majority of patients in long-term care have one or more chronic conditions. CNAs do not treat these conditions — that is the role of the physician and nurse — but CNAs observe, report, and provide daily care that directly affects how well these conditions are managed.

**Diabetes:** Diabetes is a condition in which the body cannot properly regulate blood glucose. Type 1 diabetes requires insulin. Type 2 diabetes is managed with diet, oral medications, and sometimes insulin. CNAs must monitor for hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) and hyperglycemia (high blood sugar).

Hypoglycemia signs: shakiness, sweating, confusion, pale skin, rapid heartbeat, hunger. If a patient is conscious and able to swallow, offer 4 oz of juice or glucose tablets and report immediately. If unconscious, do not give anything by mouth — call for help immediately.

Hyperglycemia signs: increased thirst and urination, fatigue, blurred vision, fruity breath odor. Report to the charge nurse. CNA responsibilities for diabetic patients include: monitoring meal intake and reporting if the patient does not eat, providing meticulous foot care (inspect daily for cuts, blisters, or redness — do not cut toenails), and reporting any skin breakdown immediately.

**COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease):** COPD is a progressive lung disease that makes breathing difficult. Patients with COPD often use supplemental oxygen. CNA responsibilities include: positioning the patient upright (Fowler's or semi-Fowler's) to maximize lung expansion, monitoring oxygen equipment and reporting any issues, encouraging pursed-lip breathing, pacing activities to prevent breathlessness, and reporting increased dyspnea, cyanosis (bluish lips or fingertips), or oxygen saturation below 90%.

**CHF (Congestive Heart Failure):** CHF occurs when the heart cannot pump blood effectively, causing fluid to back up into the lungs and body tissues. Signs include edema (swelling) in the legs and ankles, shortness of breath (especially when lying flat), rapid weight gain, and fatigue. CNA responsibilities include: daily weights at the same time each day on the same scale, monitoring fluid intake (patients may be on fluid restrictions), elevating legs to reduce edema, and reporting weight gain of 2 or more pounds in 24 hours or 5 pounds in a week.

**Stroke (CVA):** A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted. Effects depend on which part of the brain is affected and may include hemiplegia (weakness or paralysis on one side), aphasia, dysphagia, and cognitive changes. CNA responsibilities include: always approaching and assisting from the patient's stronger side during ambulation, dressing the weaker side first and undressing it last, providing communication support for patients with aphasia, and monitoring for signs of a new stroke (sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty — use FAST: Face, Arms, Speech, Time to call 911).

**Practical Application:** Students will review case studies for each condition and identify the correct CNA response, documentation, and reporting action.

## Lesson 10.2 — End-of-Life Care

**Learning Objective:** Students will describe the stages of grief, identify signs of approaching death, provide comfort-focused end-of-life care, and support patients and families during the dying process.

**Key Terms:** Palliative care, hospice, Kübler-Ross stages, Cheyne-Stokes respiration, mottling, post-mortem care, advance directive

**Instructional Content:**

Death is a natural part of life, and caring for patients at the end of life is one of the most meaningful things a CNA can do. End-of-life care is not about giving up — it is about ensuring that a person's final days are as comfortable, dignified, and peaceful as possible.

**Kübler-Ross Stages of Grief:** Elisabeth Kübler-Ross identified five stages that people commonly experience when facing death or loss: Denial ("This can't be happening"), Anger ("Why me?"), Bargaining ("If I get better, I'll..."), Depression (profound sadness and withdrawal), and Acceptance (coming to terms with the reality). These stages are not linear — a person may move back and forth between stages, skip stages, or experience multiple stages simultaneously. CNAs should not try to move a patient through the stages but should meet them where they are with compassion and presence.

**Hospice and Palliative Care:** Palliative care focuses on comfort and quality of life at any stage of illness. Hospice is a specific type of palliative care for patients who are expected to live six months or less if the illness follows its natural course. Hospice does not hasten death — it provides expert pain management, emotional support, and spiritual care for both the patient and family. CNA responsibilities in hospice include: meticulous mouth care (the mouth becomes very dry), frequent repositioning for comfort, pain assessment and reporting, and providing a calm, quiet environment.

**Signs of Approaching Death:** As death approaches, the body undergoes predictable changes. Circulation decreases, causing mottling (a bluish-purple blotchy discoloration of the skin, usually starting in the legs). Breathing becomes irregular — Cheyne-Stokes respiration is a pattern of progressively deeper breaths followed by a period of no breathing (apnea). The "death rattle" is a gurgling sound caused by secretions in the throat that the patient can no longer clear. Urine output decreases and darkens. The patient becomes unresponsive. Extremities become cool and pale.

**CNA's Role at End of Life:** Continue to provide all personal care with the same dignity and respect as always. Hearing is believed to be the last sense to go — speak to the patient as if they can hear you, because they may. Keep the environment calm and quiet. Allow family members to be present and support them. Do not leave a dying patient alone if it can be avoided. Report changes in condition to the charge nurse.

**Post-Mortem Care:** After death is pronounced by the nurse or physician, the CNA may be asked to assist with post-mortem care. This includes: bathing the body, closing the eyes and mouth, removing tubes and equipment as directed, placing the body in proper alignment, applying identification tags, and preparing the body for the family to view if requested. Treat the body with the same dignity and respect as a living patient.

**Practical Application:** Students will discuss a case study involving a patient in the final stages of life and identify the appropriate CNA responses to the patient's physical needs, the family's emotional needs, and the documentation requirements.

# MODULE 11: Documentation & Reporting

## Lesson 11.1 — Accurate Documentation and Reporting

**Learning Objective:** Students will complete an ADL flow sheet accurately, identify what must be reported immediately to the charge nurse, and describe the CNA's role in the minimum data set process.

**Key Terms:** ADL flow sheet, incident report, minimum data set (MDS), change of condition, objective vs. subjective data, military time

**Instructional Content:**

In healthcare, the rule is simple: if it wasn't documented, it wasn't done. Documentation is a legal record of the care provided and the patient's response to that care. Accurate, timely documentation protects the patient, protects the CNA, and ensures continuity of care across all shifts and all members of the healthcare team.

**Principles of Documentation:** Write legibly. Use only approved abbreviations. Document in real time or as soon as possible after care is provided — never document care before it is given. Be objective — document what you observed, not what you think it means. "Patient's skin is red and warm to touch over the sacrum" is objective. "Patient has a pressure sore" is a diagnosis — not within the CNA's scope to document. Correct errors by drawing a single line through the error, writing "error," initialing, and writing the correct information — never use correction fluid or scribble out errors.

**ADL Flow Sheets:** ADL flow sheets are used to document the patient's level of independence with activities of daily living on each shift. Common ADL categories include: bathing, dressing, grooming, oral hygiene, toileting, continence, eating, and mobility. Each category uses a standardized coding system (e.g., 1 = independent, 2 = supervision, 3 = limited assistance, 4 = extensive assistance, 5 = total dependence). Accurate ADL documentation directly affects the facility's reimbursement through the MDS process.

**What to Report Immediately:** Falls or suspected falls. Any change in vital signs outside normal range. New or worsening pain. Changes in mental status (new confusion, increased agitation). Changes in skin integrity (new redness, open areas). Changes in eating or drinking (refusal of meals, difficulty swallowing). Changes in elimination (no urine output for 4+ hours, blood in urine or stool). Patient or family complaints. Any suspected abuse or neglect.

**Minimum Data Set (MDS):** The MDS is a federally mandated comprehensive assessment tool used in nursing facilities to evaluate each resident's functional status, health conditions, and care needs. It is completed by the MDS coordinator (usually an RN) but relies heavily on CNA observations. When you document ADLs accurately and report changes in condition promptly, you are contributing directly to the MDS process and to the resident's care plan.

**Practical Application:** Students will complete a sample ADL flow sheet based on a written patient scenario, practice writing objective documentation statements, and identify which findings from a case study require immediate reporting.

# MODULE 12: Clinical Preparation & Competency Review

## Lesson 12.1 — Clinical Site Expectations and Competency Exam Preparation

**Learning Objective:** Students will demonstrate all required skills at competency level, describe clinical site expectations and professional conduct standards, and explain the format of the Indiana Nurse Aide Competency Evaluation.

**Key Terms:** Competency evaluation, Headmaster Testing Services, Indiana Nurse Aide Registry, clinical rotation, professional conduct

**Instructional Content:**

You have now completed the classroom and skills laboratory components of your CNA training. Before you enter the clinical setting, you must demonstrate that you can perform all required skills safely and competently. This is not a formality — it is a patient safety requirement. Patients in clinical facilities are real people with real health conditions, and they deserve care from a CNA who is fully prepared.

**Clinical Site Expectations:** You will be assigned to a licensed long-term care or healthcare facility for your clinical rotation. During clinical, you are a representative of Elevate for Humanity and of the nursing profession. You are expected to: arrive on time in proper uniform, introduce yourself to the charge nurse and your assigned patients, follow all facility policies and procedures, perform only the skills you have been trained and authorized to perform, report all observations and concerns to your clinical instructor immediately, maintain patient confidentiality at all times, and conduct yourself with professionalism and respect at all times.

You will be supervised by a qualified RN clinical instructor at all times. You will not be left alone with patients. Your instructor will observe your skills performance and provide feedback. If you are unsure about any aspect of a patient's care, ask your instructor before proceeding.

**Indiana Nurse Aide Competency Evaluation:** The competency evaluation is administered by Headmaster Testing Services on behalf of the Indiana State Department of Health. It consists of two parts: a written exam (70 multiple-choice questions, 90-minute time limit) and a skills demonstration (you will be asked to perform 5 randomly selected skills in front of an evaluator). You must pass both parts to be listed on the Indiana Nurse Aide Registry.

Written exam content areas: physical care skills, psychosocial care skills, role of the nurse aide, safety and emergency procedures, infection control, communication, and basic nursing skills. Skills demonstration: you will be evaluated on hand hygiene (always included) plus four additional randomly selected skills from the state's approved skills list.

**Preparation Strategies:** Review all module content. Practice every skill until it is automatic. Use the skills competency checklist to self-evaluate. Take practice written exams. Get adequate sleep the night before the exam. Arrive early. Read each question carefully before answering.

**After Certification:** Once you pass the competency evaluation, your name will be added to the Indiana Nurse Aide Registry within 30 days. You can verify your listing at the ISDH website. To maintain active status, you must work at least 8 hours of paid nursing-related work every 24 months. Your employer is required to provide at least 12 hours of in-service training per year.

**Career Pathways:** CNA certification is the first step in a healthcare career. From CNA, you can advance to: home health aide, patient care technician, medical assistant, phlebotomy technician, LPN (Licensed Practical Nurse) through an LPN bridge program, or RN through an associate or bachelor's degree program. Many healthcare employers offer tuition assistance for employees who want to advance their education.

**Practical Application:** Full skills competency check-off for all students. Each student must demonstrate hand hygiene, vital signs, bed bath, transfer, and bed making at competency level before being cleared for clinical rotation.
