# MODULE 4: Patient Rights, Ethics & Communication

## Lesson 4.1 — Resident Rights and Abuse Prevention

**Learning Objective:** Students will identify the federally mandated resident rights under OBRA 1987, recognize the types and signs of abuse and neglect, and describe the mandatory reporting process in Indiana.

**Key Terms:** OBRA 1987, resident rights, abuse, neglect, exploitation, misappropriation, mandatory reporter, Adult Protective Services

**Instructional Content:**

The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1987 (OBRA 1987) established a comprehensive set of rights for residents of nursing facilities that receive Medicare or Medicaid funding. These rights are not optional — they are federal law. Every CNA must know these rights and actively protect them in every patient interaction.

**Core Resident Rights include:**

The right to be treated with dignity and respect at all times, regardless of cognitive status, physical condition, or behavior. Dignity means addressing residents by their preferred name, knocking before entering a room, providing privacy during personal care, and never speaking about a resident as if they are not present.

The right to privacy — in personal care, in communications, and in medical information. CNAs must close curtains and doors during care, allow residents to make private phone calls, and never read a resident's mail without permission.

The right to self-determination — residents have the right to make their own decisions about their care, daily schedule, activities, and diet, even if those decisions are not what the healthcare team would recommend. A resident who refuses a bath has the right to do so. Document the refusal and report it to the charge nurse.

The right to be free from abuse, neglect, and exploitation. This is the right CNAs are most directly responsible for protecting and most directly at risk of violating.

**Types of Abuse:**

- Physical abuse: hitting, slapping, pinching, rough handling, improper use of restraints
- Verbal/emotional abuse: yelling, threatening, humiliating, ignoring, name-calling
- Sexual abuse: any non-consensual sexual contact or exposure
- Financial exploitation: taking money or property, coercing a resident to change a will
- Neglect: failure to provide necessary care — leaving a resident in a soiled brief, failing to reposition, withholding food or water
- Self-neglect: a resident's own failure to maintain adequate self-care (reported but not caused by staff)

**Signs of Abuse:** Unexplained bruises, burns, or fractures; fear or anxiety around specific staff members; withdrawal or depression; poor hygiene or weight loss inconsistent with medical condition; resident reports of mistreatment.

**Mandatory Reporting in Indiana:** Every CNA is a mandatory reporter under Indiana law. If you witness, suspect, or receive a report of abuse, neglect, or exploitation, you are legally required to report it immediately to your supervisor and, if necessary, directly to the Indiana Adult Protective Services hotline (1-800-992-6978) or the Indiana State Department of Health. Failure to report is a criminal offense. You cannot be retaliated against for making a good-faith report.

**Practical Application:** Students will review three case scenarios involving potential abuse situations and practice the correct reporting language and chain of command.

## Lesson 4.2 — Therapeutic Communication

**Learning Objective:** Students will demonstrate therapeutic communication techniques including active listening, open-ended questioning, and non-verbal communication, and adapt communication strategies for patients with cognitive, sensory, or language barriers.

**Key Terms:** Therapeutic communication, active listening, open-ended question, non-verbal communication, aphasia, validation therapy

**Instructional Content:**

Communication is the foundation of patient-centered care. As a CNA, you will communicate with patients who are frightened, confused, in pain, grieving, or angry. Your ability to respond therapeutically — in a way that promotes trust, comfort, and cooperation — directly affects the quality of care you provide.

**Therapeutic communication techniques:**

Active listening means giving your full attention to the patient — making eye contact, nodding, leaning slightly forward, and not interrupting. It means listening to understand, not just to respond. When a patient is speaking, resist the urge to formulate your reply while they are still talking.

Open-ended questions invite the patient to share more than a yes or no answer. Instead of asking "Are you in pain?" ask "Can you tell me how you're feeling today?" Instead of "Did you sleep okay?" ask "How was your night?" Open-ended questions give patients control over the conversation and often reveal important clinical information.

Reflection and restatement show the patient you heard them. "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated about not being able to walk to the bathroom on your own. Is that right?" This validates the patient's experience and confirms your understanding.

Silence is a therapeutic tool. When a patient is emotional, sitting quietly with them — without rushing to fill the silence — communicates that you are present and that their feelings matter.

Non-verbal communication includes facial expression, posture, touch, and proximity. A tense posture, crossed arms, or a rushed manner communicates impatience even if your words are kind. Approach patients at eye level when possible — pull up a chair rather than standing over a bed-bound patient.

**Communicating with Patients Who Have Dementia:** Use short, simple sentences. Speak slowly and clearly. Use the patient's name. Maintain eye contact. Approach from the front and identify yourself. Avoid arguing or correcting — if a patient believes it is 1975, do not insist it is not. Use validation therapy: acknowledge the emotion behind the statement rather than the factual content. If a patient says "I need to go pick up my children from school," respond with "It sounds like you love your children very much. Tell me about them."

**Communicating with Patients Who Have Aphasia:** Aphasia is a language disorder caused by stroke or brain injury. The patient may understand you but be unable to speak, or may speak but use wrong words. Use yes/no questions, picture boards, or writing. Never speak for the patient or finish their sentences. Give them time.

**Communicating with Patients Who Have Hearing Loss:** Face the patient directly. Speak clearly at a normal pace — do not shout. Reduce background noise. Use written communication if needed. Ensure hearing aids are in place and functioning.

**Practical Application:** Students will complete three communication role-plays: one with a patient who is refusing care, one with a patient who has dementia and is agitated, and one with a patient who has aphasia following a stroke.

# MODULE 5: Vital Signs

## Lesson 5.1 — Temperature, Pulse, and Respiration

**Learning Objective:** Students will accurately measure oral, axillary, and tympanic temperature; measure radial pulse rate, rhythm, and quality; and count respiratory rate, identifying normal ranges and reportable abnormalities.

**Key Terms:** Vital signs, temperature, pulse, respiration, tachycardia, bradycardia, tachypnea, bradypnea, pyrexia, hypothermia

**Instructional Content:**

Vital signs are objective measurements of the body's most basic functions. They provide critical information about a patient's physiological status and are often the first indicator of a change in condition. As a CNA, you will measure vital signs routinely and must be able to recognize when a reading falls outside the normal range and requires immediate reporting to the charge nurse.

**Temperature** measures the body's heat production and loss. Normal oral temperature is 97.6°F–99.6°F (36.4°C–37.6°C). Fever (pyrexia) is generally defined as a temperature above 100.4°F (38°C). Hypothermia is a temperature below 95°F (35°C).

Oral temperature measurement: Ensure the patient has not eaten, drunk, or smoked for 15 minutes. Place the thermometer probe under the tongue in the posterior sublingual pocket. Instruct the patient to close their lips (not teeth) around the probe. Wait for the device signal. Record the reading with the route noted (e.g., "98.6°F oral").

Axillary temperature measurement: Place the probe in the center of the axilla (armpit) with the patient's arm held firmly against their side. Axillary readings are approximately 1°F lower than oral. Note the route when documenting.

Tympanic temperature measurement: Gently pull the ear up and back (adults) to straighten the ear canal. Insert the probe snugly and press the scan button. Tympanic readings are fast and non-invasive, making them useful for confused or uncooperative patients.

**Pulse** is the wave of pressure created by each heartbeat, felt at an artery. The radial pulse (at the wrist, thumb side) is the most common site for CNA measurement. Normal adult pulse rate is 60–100 beats per minute. Tachycardia is a rate above 100 bpm. Bradycardia is a rate below 60 bpm.

Radial pulse measurement: Place your index and middle fingers (not your thumb — it has its own pulse) over the radial artery. Count beats for 60 seconds for accuracy, or 30 seconds and multiply by 2 if the rhythm is regular. Note rate, rhythm (regular or irregular), and strength (strong, weak, thready).

Report immediately: pulse below 60 or above 100, irregular rhythm, or a pulse that is significantly different from the patient's baseline.

**Respiration** is the process of breathing — inhalation and exhalation. One respiration equals one complete breath in and one breath out. Normal adult respiratory rate is 12–20 breaths per minute. Tachypnea is a rate above 20. Bradypnea is a rate below 12.

Respiratory rate measurement: Count respirations immediately after counting the pulse, while still appearing to count the pulse — patients who know they are being watched tend to alter their breathing pattern. Count for 60 seconds. Note rate, depth (shallow, normal, deep), and rhythm (regular or irregular). Report any labored breathing, use of accessory muscles, or abnormal sounds such as wheezing or gurgling.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice measuring temperature (oral, axillary, tympanic), radial pulse, and respiratory rate on a lab partner, recording findings on a vital signs documentation form and identifying which findings would require immediate reporting.

## Lesson 5.2 — Blood Pressure and Oxygen Saturation

**Learning Objective:** Students will accurately measure blood pressure using a manual sphygmomanometer and stethoscope, operate a pulse oximeter, and identify normal ranges and reportable values for both measurements.

**Key Terms:** Blood pressure, systolic, diastolic, hypertension, hypotension, Korotkoff sounds, sphygmomanometer, pulse oximetry, oxygen saturation

**Instructional Content:**

Blood pressure measures the force of blood against the walls of the arteries. It is recorded as two numbers: systolic pressure (the pressure when the heart contracts and pumps blood) over diastolic pressure (the pressure when the heart relaxes between beats). Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg).

Normal adult blood pressure is less than 120/80 mmHg. Hypertension (high blood pressure) is defined as 130/80 mmHg or higher. Hypotension (low blood pressure) is generally defined as below 90/60 mmHg. Both conditions require reporting to the charge nurse.

**Manual Blood Pressure Measurement Technique:**

Position the patient seated or lying down with the arm supported at heart level. The patient should have rested for at least five minutes and should not have exercised, smoked, or consumed caffeine in the past 30 minutes.

Select the correct cuff size — the bladder of the cuff should encircle 80% of the arm. A cuff that is too small will give a falsely high reading; a cuff that is too large will give a falsely low reading.

Apply the cuff to the bare upper arm with the lower edge approximately one inch above the antecubital fossa (the bend of the elbow). The artery marker on the cuff should be aligned over the brachial artery.

Locate the brachial pulse with your fingertips. Place the stethoscope diaphragm over the brachial artery. Close the valve on the bulb and inflate the cuff to approximately 30 mmHg above the point where the radial pulse disappears (typically 160–180 mmHg for most adults).

Slowly release the valve at a rate of 2–3 mmHg per second. Listen for the first Korotkoff sound — a clear tapping sound. This is the systolic pressure. Continue deflating. The point at which the sounds disappear completely is the diastolic pressure. Record both numbers (e.g., 118/76 mmHg).

Never take blood pressure on an arm with an IV, a dialysis access site, a mastectomy on that side, or a lymph node dissection. Use the opposite arm or the thigh if necessary.

**Pulse Oximetry:** A pulse oximeter measures the percentage of hemoglobin in the blood that is saturated with oxygen (SpO2). Normal SpO2 is 95–100%. Values below 90% indicate hypoxemia and require immediate reporting.

To use a pulse oximeter: ensure the patient's finger is warm and dry (cold or wet fingers give inaccurate readings). Remove nail polish if present. Place the probe on the index or middle finger. Wait for the reading to stabilize. Record both the SpO2 percentage and the pulse rate displayed.

**Practical Application:** Students will practice manual blood pressure measurement on lab partners, with the instructor verifying accuracy by taking the same measurement immediately after. Students will also practice pulse oximetry and document findings.

# MODULE 6: Personal Care Skills

## Lesson 6.1 — Bathing and Skin Care

**Learning Objective:** Students will perform a complete bed bath, partial bath, and shower assist using correct technique, maintaining patient dignity, privacy, and infection control throughout.

**Key Terms:** Bed bath, partial bath, perineal care, skin integrity, pressure injury, staging, turgor

**Instructional Content:**

Personal care is the heart of the CNA role. Bathing is not merely a hygiene task — it is an opportunity to assess the patient's skin, observe for changes in condition, provide comfort, and build a therapeutic relationship. Every bath should be performed with the patient's dignity and comfort as the primary priority.

**Principles of Personal Care:**
Always explain what you are going to do before you do it. Knock before entering. Close the door and pull the curtain. Maintain draping throughout — expose only the area you are actively washing. Use warm water (approximately 105–110°F — test on your inner wrist). Change water when it becomes cool or soiled. Work from clean to dirty areas — face first, perineum last. Wash, rinse, and dry each area thoroughly — moisture left on skin promotes breakdown.

**Complete Bed Bath Procedure:**
Gather all supplies before beginning: basin, washcloths (minimum 2), bath towel, clean gown, clean linens if needed, soap, lotion. Fill basin with warm water. Assist patient to comfortable position. Remove gown while maintaining draping with bath blanket.

Sequence: Face (no soap unless patient prefers), neck, ears, far arm, near arm, chest, abdomen, far leg, near leg, back (turn patient to side), perineum. Use a clean section of washcloth for each area. Rinse soap thoroughly — soap residue causes skin irritation. Pat dry — do not rub, especially on fragile skin.

**Skin Assessment During Bathing:** Every bath is a skin assessment. Inspect all bony prominences: heels, sacrum, coccyx, hips, elbows, shoulder blades, back of head. Look for redness that does not blanch (does not turn white when pressed) — this is a Stage 1 pressure injury and must be reported immediately. Also report any open areas, blisters, bruising, rashes, or skin tears.

**Pressure Injury Staging:**

- Stage 1: Non-blanchable redness of intact skin
- Stage 2: Partial thickness skin loss — shallow open ulcer or blister
- Stage 3: Full thickness skin loss — subcutaneous tissue visible
- Stage 4: Full thickness tissue loss — bone, tendon, or muscle exposed
- Unstageable: Base obscured by slough or eschar
- Deep Tissue Injury: Purple or maroon discoloration of intact skin

**Perineal Care:** Perineal care prevents urinary tract infections, skin breakdown, and odor. For female patients: wash from front to back (urethra to anus) using a clean section of washcloth for each stroke. Never wipe back to front. For male patients: retract foreskin if uncircumcised, clean the urethral meatus in a circular motion, replace foreskin after cleaning. For catheterized patients: clean the catheter tubing from the meatus outward approximately 4 inches.

**Practical Application:** Students will perform a complete bed bath on a mannequin, demonstrating correct sequence, draping, water temperature, skin assessment, and perineal care technique. Instructor will evaluate using the skills competency checklist.

## Lesson 6.2 — Oral Hygiene, Grooming, and Bed Making

**Learning Objective:** Students will perform oral hygiene including denture care, assist with grooming tasks, and make an occupied and unoccupied bed using correct technique.

**Key Terms:** Oral hygiene, dentures, stomatitis, alopecia, occupied bed, unoccupied bed, mitered corner

**Instructional Content:**

Oral hygiene is essential for patient comfort, nutrition, infection prevention, and dignity. Poor oral health is associated with pneumonia, cardiovascular disease, and malnutrition in older adults. CNAs provide oral care at least twice daily and after meals.

**Oral Hygiene for Patients with Natural Teeth:**
Assist the patient to a sitting position if possible. Apply toothpaste to a soft-bristled toothbrush. Brush all surfaces of the teeth — outer, inner, and chewing surfaces — using gentle circular motions. Brush the tongue to remove bacteria. Have the patient rinse with water or mouthwash and spit into an emesis basin. For patients who cannot spit, use a suction toothbrush or foam swabs moistened with water.

**Denture Care:**
Remove dentures by grasping the upper denture with a gauze pad and breaking the seal with a rocking motion. Remove the lower denture by lifting and rotating. Place dentures in a labeled denture cup. Brush all surfaces of the dentures with a denture brush and denture cleaner or mild soap — never use regular toothpaste, which is abrasive. Rinse thoroughly. Store in water or denture solution when not in use to prevent warping. Inspect the oral mucosa for sores, redness, or white patches (which may indicate candidiasis) and report findings.

**Mouth Care for Unconscious Patients:**
Position the patient on their side to prevent aspiration. Use foam swabs moistened with water or oral rinse — never use a toothbrush on an unconscious patient. Swab all surfaces of the mouth. Use suction if available. Apply lip moisturizer to prevent cracking.

**Hair Care:** Brush or comb hair gently, starting at the ends and working toward the scalp to prevent breakage. Respect the patient's preferred hairstyle. For patients with long hair, braid or secure it to prevent tangling. Shampooing in bed requires a shampoo basin, warm water, and a waterproof pad under the patient's head.

**Bed Making — Unoccupied Bed:**
Remove soiled linens by rolling them away from you (never shake — this disperses microorganisms into the air). Place soiled linens directly into the laundry bag — never on the floor. Apply the bottom sheet with the center fold at the center of the bed. Miter the corners: tuck the sheet under the mattress at the head, lift the side of the sheet to form a 45-degree angle, tuck the lower portion under the mattress, then fold the upper portion down and tuck. Apply the top sheet, blanket, and pillowcase.

**Bed Making — Occupied Bed:**
Explain the procedure to the patient. Lower the head of the bed to flat if tolerated. Loosen all linens. Turn the patient to the far side of the bed and roll the soiled bottom sheet toward the patient's back. Apply the clean bottom sheet on the near side, rolling the excess toward the patient. Turn the patient over the rolled linens to the clean side. Move to the other side, remove the soiled sheet, and pull through the clean sheet. Miter corners. Apply top linens. Raise the head of the bed to a comfortable position.

**Practical Application:** Students will make an occupied bed on a mannequin with a classmate playing the patient role, demonstrating correct technique, patient communication, and infection control throughout.
